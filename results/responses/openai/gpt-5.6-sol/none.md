## Short answer

A woodchuck could plausibly chuck **about 320 kg (700 lb) of wood per day**, with a rough range of **200–450 kg (440–990 lb)**—**if** “chuck” means moving wood with the same effort it uses to excavate a burrow.

## Basis and assumptions

The classic estimate is attributed to New York conservationist **Richard Thomas**, who reportedly measured the volume of soil removed from a woodchuck burrow and calculated that an equivalent volume of wood would weigh roughly **700 lb**.

Assumptions:

1. **“Chuck” means excavate, displace, or toss aside**, not hurl like an Olympic shot-putter.
2. The animal moves a wood volume comparable to the volume of soil excavated for a typical burrow.
3. Wood density is approximately **500–700 kg/m³**, depending on species and moisture.
4. Equivalent displaced volume is about **0.45–0.65 m³**.
5. The work occurs over construction of a substantial burrow, not necessarily every ordinary day.

A central estimate is therefore:

\[
0.53\ \text{m}^3 \times 600\ \text{kg/m}^3 \approx 320\ \text{kg}
\]

That equals approximately:

- **320 kg**
- **700 lb**
- **0.35 US short tons**
- roughly **0.5 m³**, or **18 ft³**, of medium-density wood

### Uncertainty

A defensible playful estimate is **320 ± 120 kg**, or **700 ± 260 lb**. The uncertainty is large because burrow size, wood density, moisture, terrain, and the meaning of “chuck” are poorly specified. If “chuck” strictly means throwing intact logs, the answer is likely **far less**—possibly near zero, because woodchucks are burrowing rodents, not lumberjacks.

Linguistically, *woodchuck* does not derive from chucking wood; it comes from an Indigenous name, commonly traced through Algonquian forms such as *wuchak*. Historically, the tongue twister was popularized by the 1902 song **“The Woodchuck Song”** by Robert Hobart Davis and Theodore F. Morse.

Scientifically precise conclusion: **enough to make the question memorable, but not enough to start a timber company.**
