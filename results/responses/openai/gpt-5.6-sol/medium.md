## Short answer

**Literally: approximately zero.** Woodchucks (*Marmota monax*) chiefly excavate soil rather than throw wood.

**Hypothetically: about 100 kg (220 lb) of wood per burrow-building effort**, with a plausible range of **50–200 kg (110–440 lb)**.

## Estimate

A traditional calculation often attributed to wildlife biologist Richard Thomas begins with the claim that a woodchuck may displace roughly **700 lb (318 kg) of soil** while digging a burrow. Treating “chuck” as “move or toss aside once”:

1. **Excavated soil mass:** 318 kg  
2. **Assumed loose-soil density:** 1,600 kg/m³  
3. **Equivalent volume:**  
   \[
   V=\frac{318}{1600}\approx0.20\ \text{m}^3
   \]
4. **Assumed average wood density:** 500 kg/m³  
5. **Wood occupying that volume:**  
   \[
   m=0.20\times500\approx100\ \text{kg}
   \]

Thus:

\[
\boxed{100\ \text{kg}\approx220\ \text{lb}}
\]

That is about **0.20 m³**, or **7.1 ft³**, of medium-density wood.

## Assumptions and uncertainty

- **Time frame:** one substantial burrow excavation, not per hour or over the animal’s lifetime.
- **“Chuck”:** move wood out of the way once, not repeatedly throw the same pieces.
- **Soil displaced:** nominally 700 lb; actual burrows vary considerably.
- **Soil density:** approximately 1,200–2,000 kg/m³.
- **Wood density:** approximately 350–800 kg/m³, depending on species and moisture.
- **Estimated uncertainty:** roughly a factor of two, giving **50–200 kg (110–440 lb)**.
- **Biological caveat:** an actual woodchuck lacks the anatomy and motivation to process logs, so its observed wood-chucking output is effectively **0 kg**.

Linguistically, *woodchuck* is probably derived from an Indigenous name such as Algonquian **wuchak**, not from the words *wood* and *chuck*. The famous question is therefore a pun built on folk reinterpretation—not a forgotten lumber-industry productivity standard.
