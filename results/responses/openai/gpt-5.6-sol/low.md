## Short answer

A real woodchuck cannot chuck wood in any systematic sense. Under the traditional counterfactual—equating wood moved with the material excavated for a typical burrow—a woodchuck would chuck approximately:

- **320 kg of wood**
- **700 lb**, or **0.35 US short tons**
- At an assumed wood density of **500 kg/m³**, about **0.64 m³** or **22 ft³** of wood

A defensible uncertainty range is roughly **150–600 kg** (**330–1,300 lb**), because burrow size, soil conditions, wood density, and the meaning of *chuck* are poorly constrained.

## Assumptions and calculation

1. **“Chuck” means move or eject**, not necessarily throw ballistically.
2. **One chucking episode equals excavating one typical burrow.**
3. The traditional estimate attributed to wildlife biologist **Richard Thomas** is about **700 lb of excavated material**.
4. Representative air-dry wood density is assumed to be **500 kg/m³**.

Conversions:

\[
700\ \text{lb}\times0.453592=318\ \text{kg}\approx320\ \text{kg}
\]

\[
V=\frac{m}{\rho}=\frac{318}{500}\approx0.64\ \text{m}^3\approx22\ \text{ft}^3
\]

If all that wood were raised or thrown an average vertical distance of one metre, the minimum mechanical work would be:

\[
E=mgh\approx318(9.81)(1)\approx3.1\ \text{kJ}
\]

Actual metabolic expenditure would be several times greater.

## Linguistic and historical caveat

*Woodchuck* is not literally a compound meaning “wood thrower.” It derives from an Indigenous Algonquian name, often represented as *wuchak*, for the animal also called the **groundhog** (*Marmota monax*). The familiar wording became popular through the 1902 “Woodchuck Song,” associated with lyricist Robert Hobart Davis and composer Theodore F. Morse.

Thus, the question is linguistically a pun, physically underdetermined, and zoologically unfair.

**Sources:** Richard Thomas’s widely cited burrow-displacement estimate, reported in *Scientific American* in 1988; Merriam-Webster’s etymology of *woodchuck*; Robert Hobart Davis and Theodore F. Morse, “The Woodchuck Song” (1902).
