## Short answer

A real woodchuck—*Marmota monax*—does not habitually chuck wood, so the observed answer is approximately **0 kilograms**.

Under the traditional counterfactual, however, a woodchuck could chuck about **320 kg (700 lb)** of wood during the effort required to excavate a typical burrow.

## Assumptions and calculation

The often-quoted estimate is attributed to New York wildlife biologist **Richard Thomas**, who reasoned from the amount of material a woodchuck moves while digging its burrow.[1] This is a humorous extrapolation, not a controlled measurement.

Assume:

1. **“Chuck” means move or throw aside**, rather than launch a specified distance.
2. The time interval is **one substantial burrow-excavation project**, not per hour or per lifetime.
3. The animal handles wood at roughly the same total mass rate as excavated soil.
4. Representative dry wood has a density near **500 kg/m³**.

Then:

- Mass: approximately **320 kg**
- Imperial mass: approximately **700 lb**
- Equivalent wood volume:  
  \[
  V=\frac{m}{\rho}=\frac{320\ \mathrm{kg}}{500\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}}
  \approx0.64\ \mathrm{m^3}
  \]
- Imperial volume: approximately **23 ft³**, or about **0.85 cubic yard**

That is roughly a loosely stacked, woodchuck-sized pile of logs—and an immediate violation of several workplace-safety recommendations.

## Uncertainty

A reasonable deliberately broad estimate is:

- **160–640 kg**
- **350–1,400 lb**
- About **0.3–1.3 m³** or **11–45 ft³** of average-density wood

The uncertainty is at least a factor of two because burrow size, soil conditions, wood density, throwing distance, motivation, and the woodchuck’s contractual definition of “chuck” are unspecified.

Linguistically, the joke is especially unfair: *woodchuck* is probably derived from an Algonquian animal name such as *wuchak*, not from the English words *wood* and *chuck*.[2]
