## Short answer

A woodchuck would chuck **about 320 kg (700 lb) of wood**, with a highly speculative range of **160–640 kg (350–1,400 lb)**.

## Reasoning

The tongue twister has no scientific answer because woodchucks—also called groundhogs (*Marmota monax*)—do not actually chuck wood. The classic estimate is attributed to wildlife biologist **Richard Thomas**, who reportedly reasoned from the amount of soil displaced when a woodchuck excavates a burrow: roughly **700 lb**.

### Assumptions

1. **“Chuck” means displace, excavate, or toss**, not chew or consume.
2. A hypothetical woodchuck handles a volume of wood comparable to the volume of soil removed for a typical burrow.
3. The quoted soil mass and the equivalent wood mass are treated loosely; actual conversion depends strongly on:
   - burrow size,
   - soil density,
   - wood species and moisture,
   - whether “chucking” means moving whole pieces, chips, or sawdust.
4. Because the premise is counterfactual and the historical estimate is informal, a **factor-of-two uncertainty** is more honest than a precise error bar.
