## Short answer

Under the traditional counterfactual assumptions, a woodchuck would chuck about **318 kg (700 lb)** of wood—roughly **0.32 metric tonnes or 0.35 US short tons**—during the effort equivalent to excavating one typical burrow.

A defensible uncertainty range is approximately **160–480 kg (350–1,050 lb)**. This is a playful engineering estimate, not a zoological measurement.

## Assumptions and method

1. **“Chuck” means move or throw once**, rather than chew, split, or repeatedly transport.
2. The quantity refers to **one burrow-building episode**; the question supplies no time interval.
3. A hypothetical wood-chucking woodchuck can move roughly the same total mass of wood as the soil it displaces while digging.
4. Wood is in manageable pieces; log size, moisture, density, terrain, and labor regulations are unspecified.
5. The often-quoted **700 lb** estimate is attributed to wildlife biologist Richard Thomas, who reportedly based it on the soil excavated from a typical woodchuck burrow.[2]

Conversion:

\[
700\ \text{lb}\times0.45359237=317.5\ \text{kg}\approx318\ \text{kg}.
\]

If that mass were cumulatively lifted through one vertical metre, the minimum useful mechanical work would be about:

\[
E=mgh\approx318(9.81)(1)\approx3.1\ \text{kJ}.
\]

Actual energy expenditure would be substantially greater because digging, friction, carrying, and dramatic comic timing are inefficient.

The **±50% uncertainty** is judgmental rather than statistical. Burrows vary, and wood density and chucking geometry matter considerably.[4]

## Linguistic and historical footnotes

The sentence is a **counterfactual conditional**: actual woodchucks do not characteristically chuck wood, and neither “chuck” nor the duration is operationally defined. Thus it has no unique scientific answer.

Moreover, *woodchuck* is an English adaptation of an Algonquian animal name, not a compound meaning “an animal employed to throw timber.” The famous wording was popularized by Robert Hobart Davis and Theodore F. Morse’s 1902 **“The Woodchuck Song.”**[1]

So the rigorous answer is: **indeterminate**.  
The conventional entertaining answer is: **about 700 pounds**.

That is approximately the mass of a small grand piano, although a piano-chucking woodchuck would require a different grant proposal.
