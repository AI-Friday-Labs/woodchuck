A woodchuck would chuck **about 1 cubic metre of wood**—if “chuck” means *move aside the same volume it excavates while digging a burrow*, rather than literally hurling logs.

### Assumptions
1. **Animal:** a woodchuck/groundhog (*Marmota monax*).
2. **Meaning of “chuck”:** displace material during burrow excavation.
3. **Burrow volume:** about **35 ft³**, a commonly cited estimate.
4. **Wood density:** moderately light-to-average dry wood, about **600 kg/m³**. Actual wood varies greatly by species and moisture.

### Calculation
\[
35\ \text{ft}^3 \times 0.0283168\ \frac{\text{m}^3}{\text{ft}^3}
= 0.991\ \text{m}^3
\]

So the equivalent wood volume is:

- **0.99 m³**, approximately **1.0 m³**
- **35 ft³**

At a representative density of \(600\ \text{kg/m}^3\):

\[
0.991\ \text{m}^3 \times 600\ \text{kg/m}^3
\approx 595\ \text{kg}
\]

That is:

- **about 600 kg of wood**
- **about 1,300 lb of wood**
- or roughly **0.65 short tons** / **0.59 metric tonnes**

### Uncertainty
This is a deliberately playful estimate, not a measurement of log-throwing capacity.

If burrow volume ranges from roughly **0.6–1.4 m³** and wood density from **400–800 kg/m³**, the equivalent mass could plausibly be:

- **240–1,120 kg**
- **530–2,470 lb**
- while the volume remains on the order of **20–50 ft³**.

The famous answer of **700 lb** corresponds to about **318 kg**, which assumes very light, dry wood—around **320 kg/m³** or **20 lb/ft³**—rather than typical hardwood.

Literally, woodchucks are specialized diggers, not lumber tossers, so the experimentally observed answer for “how much wood can one throw?” is effectively **unknown—and probably close to zero logs**.

### Historical note
The line is an American folk tongue twister rather than a scientific question. Wildlife biologist Richard Thomas famously gave the traditional estimate of **700 lb** in a 1988 humorous analysis based on the amount of earth displaced by a typical woodchuck burrow.

**References**
- Thomas, R. J. (1988). “How Much Wood Could a Woodchuck Chuck? A Study in Animal Behavior.” *Wildlife Society Bulletin*, 16(4), 440–444.
- U.S. Forest Products Laboratory. *Wood Handbook: Wood as an Engineering Material*—for the large variation in wood density by species and moisture content.
