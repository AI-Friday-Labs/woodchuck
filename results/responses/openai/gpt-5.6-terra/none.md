A classic 1988 wildlife study by Richard Thomas estimated that a woodchuck (groundhog, *Marmota monax*) could move about **700 lb of dirt** while excavating a burrow. The tongue twister substitutes wood for dirt, so the usual playful answer is:

> A woodchuck would chuck as much wood as a woodchuck could chuck, if a woodchuck could chuck wood.

## Estimate

### Assumptions
1. A woodchuck’s “chucking” capacity is approximated by the mass it can excavate in building a burrow.
2. The published estimate of excavated soil is approximately **700 lb** per burrow.
3. Wood is assumed to be loose, movable pieces rather than a solid tree trunk.
4. “How much” refers to a comparable total mass over a burrow-building episode, not necessarily per hour or per day.

### Result
- **Imperial:** approximately **700 lb of wood**
- **SI:** approximately **318 kg of wood**

Conversion:

\[
700\ \text{lb}\times 0.45359237\ \frac{\text{kg}}{\text{lb}}
\approx 317.5\ \text{kg}
\]

So a reasonable answer is:

\[
\boxed{\text{About } 700\text{ lb, or }318\text{ kg, of wood.}}
\]

## Uncertainty

This is not a direct measurement of wood-moving behavior. Groundhogs dig dirt; they do not normally cut, split, or transport firewood. Therefore the uncertainty is large.

A defensible rough range is:

- **300–1,400 lb**
- **140–640 kg**

This roughly allows for variation in burrow size, soil density, individual body size, wood piece size, terrain, motivation, and the substantial absurdity of asking a rodent to work as a lumberjack.

If “chuck” means **throw**, rather than merely move, the answer would likely be much lower, since lifting and tossing wood requires more force than pushing soil aside.

## Historical and linguistic note

The phrase is a tongue twister, often attributed in popular culture to American entertainer Robert H. Davis, who used it in an early twentieth-century song. “Woodchuck” is a common name for the North American groundhog. Despite the name, woodchucks are not primarily associated with wood; the word likely derives through Algonquian-language forms rather than literally meaning “a chucking animal of wood.”
