# openai/gpt-5.6-terra - low

## The short answer

A commonly quoted estimate is:

**A woodchuck would chuck about 700 lb of wood**  
= **about 320 kg**  
= roughly **0.4–0.5 m³** of typical hardwood, depending on moisture and species.

This is not a measurement of actual wood-throwing—woodchucks (*Marmota monax*) dig soil rather than toss logs—but it is a reasonable humorous extrapolation from the amount of earth they excavate while making a burrow.

## Assumptions and calculation

I interpret **“chuck”** as *move or eject material while excavating*, not as *throw a firewood log across a field*.

### Burrow model

Assume a typical burrow tunnel is approximately:

- Length: **35 ft** = **10.7 m**
- Diameter: **6 in** = **0.152 m**
- Shape: roughly cylindrical
- Excavated soil bulk density: **100 lb/ft³** = **1,600 kg/m³**

Tunnel volume:

\[
V=\pi r^2L
\]

\[
V=\pi(0.25\ \text{ft})^2(35\ \text{ft})\approx 6.9\ \text{ft}^3
\]

Soil mass:

\[
6.9\ \text{ft}^3 \times 100\ \text{lb/ft}^3
\approx 690\ \text{lb}
\]

Rounded for the proverb and for the extra chambers, entrances, and general woodland exaggeration:

\[
\boxed{\text{about 700 lb}}
\]

or

\[
\boxed{\text{about 320 kg}}
\]

## What does that mean in wood?

Wood density varies greatly.

| Wood condition | Approx. density | Wood volume equivalent to 320 kg / 700 lb |
|---|---:|---:|
| Light, dry softwood | 400 kg/m³ | 0.80 m³ / 28 ft³ |
| Typical seasoned hardwood | 600 kg/m³ | 0.53 m³ / 19 ft³ |
| Dense or green hardwood | 800 kg/m³ | 0.40 m³ / 14 ft³ |

Thus, if supplied with conveniently sized, chuckable wood, a woodchuck might be credited with moving approximately:

\[
\boxed{0.4\text{–}0.8\ \text{m}^3}
\]

of wood, or:

\[
\boxed{14\text{–}28\ \text{ft}^3}
\]

depending on the wood.

## Uncertainty

This estimate is uncertain by at least a factor of about two.

\[
\boxed{700 \pm 350\ \text{lb}}
\]

or

\[
\boxed{320 \pm 160\ \text{kg}}
\]

Why?

- Burrows vary hugely in length, depth, and number of chambers.
- Soil density can range from loose sandy earth to compact clay.
- “Chuck” could mean push, drag, carry, toss, or ceremonially launch.
- Actual woodchucks have claws optimized for digging, not for lumber logistics.
- A wet oak round and a dry pine stick are very different opponents.

A rigorous physicist’s answer is therefore: **the result depends on the woodchuck’s available energy, grip, motivation, wood dimensions, and union contract.**

## Linguistic note

Despite appearances, **woodchuck** is not traditionally understood as “an animal that chucks wood.” The name likely entered English through an Indigenous North American language—often connected with Cree or Ojibwe forms such as *wucak* or *otchek*—for the animal now known as the groundhog. The tongue twister works because English speakers reanalyzed *woodchuck* as *wood + chuck*.

## Historical note

The “700 pounds” answer is widely associated with a late-twentieth-century estimate attributed to New York wildlife professional Richard Thomas, who reasoned from the amount of dirt a woodchuck might remove while burrowing. It has since become the standard semi-serious answer to the classic riddle.
