### 1. Historical & Linguistic Context
Historically, the tongue twister was published in 1902 by Richard Thomas. However, the linguist in me must rudely interrupt the premise: the word "woodchuck" is an anglicization of the Algonquian (specifically Narragansett) word *wuchak*, which means "the digger." It has absolutely nothing to do with wood or chucking. But we are not here to ruin the joke with etymology, we are here to do science.

### 2. The Physics & Mathematics of "Chucking"
To calculate the chucking volume, we must consult the literature. In 1988, wildlife biologist Richard Thomas (not the 1902 guy, history is funny like that) calculated that a burrowing woodchuck moves approximately 35 cubic feet of dirt to create its den. 

**Assumptions:**
1. "Chucking" entails displacing an object approximately 1.5 meters out of a hole.
2. The woodchuck's motivation to chuck wood is equal to its biological imperative to dig a burrow.
3. We are substituting the volume of dirt displaced for an equivalent volume of average-density wood (e.g., seasoned oak).
4. The woodchuck does not burn out, unionize, or file for workers' compensation.

**The Calculation:**
* Volume of dirt moved = 35 cubic feet.
* Density of seasoned oak wood = approx. 45 pounds per cubic foot ($720 \text{ kg/m}^3$).
* Total Mass = Volume $\times$ Density.
* Total Mass = 35 ft$^3 \times$ 45 lbs/ft$^3$ = 1,575 lbs.

**Estimate of Uncertainty:**
Wood density varies wildly (from balsa to lignum vitae), and rodent biomechanical efficiency isn't perfectly mapped to timber. Therefore, I assign an uncertainty of **$\pm 20\%$**. 

### 3. The Final Answer (in SI and Imperial Units)
If a woodchuck could chuck wood, it would chuck:
* **Imperial:** 1,575 lbs of wood ($\pm 315$ lbs), or roughly 35 cubic feet.
* **SI:** 714.4 kilograms of wood ($\pm 142.9$ kg), or roughly 0.99 cubic meters.
