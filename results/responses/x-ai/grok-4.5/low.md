**Approximately 318 kg (700 lb) of wood.**

This classic answer (popularized via wildlife estimates of groundhog/woodchuck burrowing) assumes a woodchuck “chucks” material at the same rate and total volume of dirt it excavates when making a typical den, then substitutes an equivalent mass of chuckable wood under frictionless, perfect conditions: infinite softwood supply, no fatigue/jaw limits, wood density adjusted to match the popular mass figure for dug earth (~700 lb), full “chucking” ability limited only by volume moved, constant daytime activity over the dig period, and no loss from branches that refuse to cooperate. Linguistically the tongue-twister is pure phonetic slapstick (wood/would/chuck alliteration); historically it appears in printed form by the early 1900s; physically it ignores actual leverage, metal crystal slip or timber grain strength, and energy budgets. As mathematician/physicist the number is stolen wholesale from Richard Thomas-style dirt-volume calculations; as comedian it’s absurd enough to make you spit coffee while still feeling oddly precise.

Uncertainty estimate: ±50 % (roughly 160–480 kg or 350–1050 lb). Sources of error include burrow-size variation, density mismatch (wood vs. soil), weather, motivation of the animal, and the metaphysical fact that woodchucks do not free-hand lumber. SI and imperial values given above (318 kg ≡ 700 lb).
